#### Process

Above Average

The processes that new portfolio managers Dan Kilbride and Richard Lin have used independently are distinct but not divergent. Kelley has sought three kinds of growth stocks: high-quality growth firms; fast-growers with unique products or disruptive technology; and opportunistic purchases of cheap growth companies and those hitting positive inflection points. Meanwhile, Lin has a more focused approach that emphasizes long-term durability and reasonable valuations. He targets firms with competitive advantages benefiting from secular trends with the potential to carve out market share. If those thrusts sound different, the managers think that’s good. Lin notes that confirming information is useless, so disagreement is important.

While their techniques have distinct tacks, their core principles of identifying durable growth businesses without overpaying for them are the same. So, avoiding companies with unmanageable debt and isolating those with high returns on invested capital are key to both. Both managers also exhibit the mettle to stick with a high-conviction investment thesis under pressure but the awareness to avoid anchoring to the past when the facts around an investment thesis change.

Some aspects of portfolio construction seem likely to organically remain the same. While Richard Lin tends to buy and hold, averaging 28% turnover since 2021 on his other charge (Affinity OTC), Dan Kilbride trades more, averaging 84% in Affinity Diversified Stock. The midpoint of those approaches would be 56%, pretty close to this strategy’s 48% average over the past three years. Similarly, as of early 2025, Lin stuffs a hefty 65% of assets into his top 10 holdings, while Kelley devotes just 37% to them; the average is 51%, almost identical to the 50% figure here under Jason Frampton and Ash Anolik.

Other aspects of the portfolio may change unless the managers adjust their tendencies. This strategy has an all-cap benchmark, so in early 2025 it had a 6% stake in small caps. Kelley’s main charge had just 3% in small-caps and Lin's 1%. Previous managers Weiner and Anolik tended to have double-digit weightings in international fare, recently 13%. That’s much higher than Kelley’s 7% and Lin’s 5%.

Ultimately, though, the strategy will be about picking the right stocks. The duo has a decision to make about Alphabet: Kelley’s main fund recently had a 3.5% stake while Lin’s was 10%. Kelley notes that artificial intelligence has lowered barriers to entry in search, potentially eroding Alphabet’s cash flow. Lin adds that the firm is at the forefront of AI, so it might leverage the technology to maintain its business edge. How well the duo debates and decides will be crucial to success.
