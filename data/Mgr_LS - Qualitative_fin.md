#### Process

Manager Abraham Heston thinks and acts like a long-term investor. His team's "alpha thesis" (or its view of what gives it an edge over the competition) is sound in principle. It holds that patient, high-conviction, price-sensitive investing in companies with clear and enduring competitive advantages is key to success. Here, the focus is on stocks in the Russell 1000 Growth Index and a handful of large- and mid-cap non-US companies.

The team then uses an extensive seven-step process to identify effective business models that can produce reliable free cash flow growth. Analysts dig into company and industry growth drivers and expect them to last five years or longer. They also try to discern whether a stock's recent price reflects changes in market sentiment or fundamentals, looking for opportunities where the two disconnect. They often spend months on their research. Ultimately, Heston constructs a relatively concentrated 30- to 40-stock portfolio that he trades with restraint. Companies with identified competitive edges usually dominate the list. While he has few formal diversification requirements, Heston typically distributes holdings across a healthy variety of sectors and industries.

This Lanefield Sachs team regularly invests in competitively advantaged companies. Morningstar’s Economic Moat Rating is designed to capture such strengths, and every stock in the US mutual fund’s October 2025 portfolio had either a wide or narrow moat by that measure. That’s often the case here. Yet, manager Abraham Heston will consider companies facing challenges that he thinks are temporary. He entered Nike in mid-2024, for example, just after a historic drop in its share price. Additions to Tesla, Meta Platforms, and Netflix during 2022’s growth-stock selloff have since looked prescient.

Heston used to cap individual positions at 8% of assets, but some companies became even larger portions of key benchmarks. Therefore, as of mid-2024, the US mutual fund’s biggest positions may range up to 8% of assets or 5 percentage points above their weightings in either the S&P 500 or Russell 1000 Growth Index. (Cross-border versions of this strategy keep top positions under 10% of assets apiece.) Heston hasn’t abused this new freedom. Without purchasing many (if any) new shares, the US mutual fund’s Nvidia stake grew to 11.6% of assets in October 2025, but that was still below Nvidia’s 13.7% share of the Russell 1000 Growth Index that month. The portfolio was light on Microsoft (4.6% versus 11.1%) and didn’t hold Apple at all; conversely, it had relatively heavy stakes in Tesla (8.3% versus 3.9%) as well as Netflix and Oracle.

### Martifis Investment Managers abandons plan to join forces with General Associates.



Mark Doby

Senior Principal

#### Parent

Average

In January 2025, Martifis IM and General Associates announced their intention to establish a joint venture between their respective asset management operations. But over the next few months, the plan became vehemently criticized, then formally opposed, by the Italian government, as well as by two of General Associates’s largest shareholders. In September 2025, Martifis IM and General Associates agreed to remove the EUR 50 million breakup fee stipulated in the initial agreement, and by mid-December, both firms announced that they had terminated negotiations.

While the joint venture ultimately faced political roadblocks, there are a host of other reasons why a merger of this size and complexity could have been challenging. By joining forces with General Associates, Martifis IM had hoped to build critical scale, but asset manager mergers usually pursue that objective by fully integrating their investment teams and rationalizing their products to avoid duplication. Martifis IM, meanwhile, has historically afforded its subsidiaries almost complete autonomy in terms of investment processes, hiring decisions, and operations. This has allowed the distinctive investment culture of each affiliate to shine: stalwarts like Lanefield Sachs and Harrison Partners (which manages the Oakstream Investments), for example, are true gems in Martifis IM’s lineup. At other times, this hands-off structure was less successful; for example, it proved insufficient to detect or prevent the significant failings that took place at affiliate H2O, a firm that Martifis IM is currently divesting from.

As the joint venture with General Associates is now off the table, Martifis IM may begin seeking partners elsewhere. That bears watching, as prolonged periods of uncertainty can be detrimental to team stability or distract investment staff from achieving the best investor outcomes. Martifis retains its Parent rating of Average for now.
