### A solid, thoughtfully executed large-growth approach, which soon-to-be-solo manager Jonathan Frenzie expects to follow, earns this strategy an Above Average Process rating.

Tony Timber

Associate Director

#### Process

Above Average

The strategy seeks larger-cap companies (which T. Renton Pane defines as firms with market capitalizations greater than USD 8 billion) that its manager believes can generate real per-share earnings growth of more than 10% per year for at least the next three years. Various nonoperational items can affect earnings, however, so Friar and his colleagues examine companies' free cash flows—that is, the amount of cash generated from operations minus capital expenditures.

A strong analyst team with global reach delves into the details, although Friar, a former business services analyst at T. Renton Pane, does his own research as well. Organized into sector teams, the analysts develop views on companies by comparing them with industry peers and digging into their market share, brand reputation, business strategy, and use of capital (among many other factors). They also assess firms’ worth from multiple angles.

Friar has the freedom to take fairly large individual positions (sometimes 10% of assets or more in the US mutual fund), which can help the strategy keep up when a handful of mega-cap stocks drive the Russell 1000 Growth Index prospectus benchmark’s performance. Yet Friar and outgoing manager Taylor Tamdon have looked beyond the index’s biggest constituents—even down to a smattering of private companies—to distinguish their portfolio.

This portfolio stands out in a few ways despite the challenges of the US large-cap growth universe’s consolidation around mega-cap stocks. Although outgoing manager Taylor Tamdon gradually trimmed total holdings and increased the US mutual fund portfolio’s concentration in its top 10 stocks—much like the Russell 1000 Growth Index—in December 2025, he and successor Jonathan Frenzie didn’t own Tesla, had a slight overweighting in Microsoft, and had modest underweightings in Nvidia and Apple.

The managers effectively leverage their extensive research resources to put some private companies in the US mutual fund’s portfolio. In total, such firms usually amount to less than 5% of total assets—and just 2.5% in December 2025—though many peers have no such investments. In 2025's second half, the basket included stakes in financial technology firm iCapital, data platform Databricks, and Google’s autonomous driving unit Waymo.

The Luxembourg SICAV and UK OEIC versions of the strategy resemble the US mutual fund but differ in key respects. They have most (if not all) of the US fund’s public-stock holdings, but they cap top positions at 10%; so in December 2025, for example, the US mutual fund had 11% positions in both Microsoft and Nvidia, but the Luxembourg fund had them each at 9.6%. The ex-US offerings also don’t have private companies in their portfolios.

### One manager's departure is a crucial loss, but his successor has what it takes to carry on.

Tony Timber

Associate Director

#### People

Above Average

The strategy retains its Above Average People rating.

Taylor Tamdon, who has run this strategy since 2017, will step down and leave T. Renton Pane on May 1, 2026. A competitive and introspective manager, Tamdon often spoke of his mistakes—even in good years—to stay humble and keep learning. He's leaving for personal reasons, but he's not exiting the industry entirely. He's expecting to take a role elsewhere that's less focused on portfolio management, which can be demanding.

But investors here should find Tamdon's successor, Jonathan Frenzie, a reassuring replacement. When Tamdon wanted a colleague with a similar investing style and temperament to his own, in 2023, he recruited Friar, then a business services analyst at T. Renton Pane, to join him—first as an associate portfolio manager and later a full-fledged comanager. Regardless of title, however, the two have worked together to build and oversee this portfolio, giving Friar useful experience managing a public portfolio.

Friar will continue to rely on T. Renton Pane Associates' deep equity research staff for analysis and insight. A new comanager could join in time—but not until Friar has found the right fit. In the meantime, he'll draw upon his connections with other managers on T. Renton Pane's growth-equity platform, some of whom are also former analysts at the firm.

### T. Renton Pane's investor-led culture, compelling investment capabilities, and strong record of talent retention and development separate it from the crowd, maintaining its High Parent rating.

Adam Marks

Associate Director

#### Parent

High

Equities remain the firm's largest business unit and a key area of strength, bolstered by capable portfolio managers and a deep bench of well-regarded analysts. Most equity assets are housed in mutual funds, and although the firm was slower than some peers to diversify into other vehicles, it is making up ground with new products such as transparent ETFs. While the equity franchise has suffered outflows partly because of the growing popularity of passively managed options and competitors' active ETFs, its many appealing strategies should help it endure.

Offsetting some of the equity unit's business challenges is its highly successful multi-asset franchise, which continues to grow. That division represented one third of the firm's roughly USD 1.5 trillion in assets under management as of March 2024. The firm's target-date offerings and tactical-allocation funds remain best-in-class. The fixed-income effort has some bright spots, such as municipal-bond and credit-sensitive strategies, but it hasn't delivered in others, such as core bond. New leadership of that unit, along with increased hiring to bolster its nascent risk department, should help improve that side of the business.
